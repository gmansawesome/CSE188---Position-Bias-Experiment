Equipment review procedures at an environmental testing laboratory are intended to ensure that instruments, support equipment, and associated systems remain suitable for their intended analytical and operational purposes. The review process typically applies to equipment used for sample preparation, chemical analysis, microbiological testing, physical measurements, data capture, storage, and environmental control. It also includes ancillary devices that may influence the validity of results, such as balances, pipettes, thermometers, refrigerators, incubators, fume hoods, water purification units, and automated samplers. The procedures are designed to provide a documented basis for decisions on equipment use, maintenance, calibration, repair, replacement, and retirement.

The review of equipment is usually incorporated into the laboratory’s quality management system. It supports compliance with regulatory requirements, accreditation standards, client specifications, and internal quality objectives. Environmental testing laboratories often operate under requirements derived from ISO/IEC 17025, national environmental regulations, state or regional certification programs, and method-specific criteria. Because test results may be used for regulatory reporting, site remediation decisions, drinking water compliance, waste characterization, or public health assessments, the reliability of equipment is a central operational concern.

A complete equipment review procedure begins with identification and classification of equipment. Each item that can affect test results is assigned a unique identifier and entered into an equipment inventory. The inventory normally records the manufacturer, model, serial number, location, date received, date placed into service, responsible department, calibration status, maintenance requirements, software or firmware version where applicable, and any restrictions on use. The inventory is periodically checked against equipment physically present in the laboratory. Discrepancies are investigated and corrected to maintain traceability.

Classification is used to determine the level of review required. Critical analytical instruments, such as gas chromatographs, mass spectrometers, inductively coupled plasma instruments, ion chromatographs, total organic carbon analyzers, and spectrophotometers, generally require the most detailed review. Equipment used for environmental control or sample integrity, such as refrigerators and freezers, is also reviewed because temperature excursions can affect sample acceptability. Less complex devices, including stir plates, timers, and hot plates, may be subject to a simpler review if their performance has limited direct effect on reported results. The classification should be justified and documented.

Before new equipment is placed into service, the laboratory conducts an acceptance review. This review confirms that the equipment received matches the purchase specification and that required documentation is available. Documentation may include manuals, certificates of calibration, installation records, service reports, software licenses, safety information, and manufacturer performance specifications. The equipment is inspected for damage, completeness, proper utilities, and compatibility with the laboratory environment. If installation by a vendor is required, the vendor’s installation report is retained as part of the equipment file.

Acceptance review also includes verification that the equipment can perform as required for the laboratory’s intended applications. For analytical instruments, this may involve installation qualification, operational qualification, and performance qualification. These activities confirm that the instrument is installed correctly, operates according to specified parameters, and produces acceptable results for relevant test methods. For example, a gas chromatograph may be evaluated for retention time stability, detector response, resolution, carryover, and calibration behavior. A balance may be assessed for readability, repeatability, eccentricity, and calibration traceability. The degree of testing is based on the equipment type and the risk associated with its use.

Calibration review is a major component of equipment control. Each item requiring calibration is assigned a calibration interval based on manufacturer recommendations, method requirements, regulatory expectations, frequency of use, historical performance, and risk. Calibration may be performed by the laboratory, by an approved external provider, or by the manufacturer. Calibration providers are reviewed to confirm technical competence and traceability to recognized standards. Certificates are examined for the identity of the equipment, calibration date, due date or interval, measurement results, uncertainties where applicable, standards used, traceability statements, and any limitations.

The laboratory should define how calibration results are evaluated. It is not sufficient to confirm that a certificate was received. The results must be compared with acceptance criteria relevant to the equipment’s use. If a thermometer is used to monitor a sample storage refrigerator with an allowable range of 0 to 6 degrees Celsius, the calibration uncertainty and correction factor must be considered when determining whether readings are acceptable. If a balance is used for weighing small quantities of standards, the calibration and verification results must support the required measurement tolerance. The review decision should be documented, including any adjustments, restrictions, or follow-up actions.

Between scheduled calibrations, many laboratories perform intermediate checks. These checks provide evidence that equipment remains in control during routine use. Examples include daily balance checks using certified weights, continuing calibration verification for analytical instruments, temperature checks for ovens and refrigerators, pipette volume checks, and instrument tune checks. The frequency of intermediate checks should reflect equipment stability and method requirements. Acceptance criteria must be stated in advance. When results fall outside criteria, the equipment is removed from service or restricted until the issue is evaluated.

Preventive maintenance review is also required. Instruments used in environmental testing are often exposed to complex matrices, including wastewater, soil extracts, sludge, industrial discharges, and air sampling media. These matrices may introduce contamination, cause wear, or degrade system performance. Preventive maintenance is intended to reduce unexpected failures and maintain consistent analytical conditions. Maintenance activities may include replacing seals, liners, septa, pump tubing, filters, lamps, columns, detector components, vacuum pump oil, autosampler parts, and reagent lines. For support equipment, maintenance may include cleaning, lubrication, inspection of electrical cords, verification of airflow, defrosting, and condenser cleaning.

Maintenance schedules are established using a combination of manufacturer guidance, method experience, usage levels, and observed performance. The review procedure should require documentation of completed maintenance, including date, person performing the work, parts replaced, observations, and any tests completed before returning the equipment to service. Vendor service records are retained and reviewed for completeness. If maintenance reveals recurring problems, the laboratory evaluates whether additional controls are needed, such as revised maintenance frequency, operator training, replacement parts of different specification, or eventual equipment replacement.

Equipment review is linked to method performance. Analytical methods often contain requirements for calibration curves, blanks, laboratory control samples, matrix spikes, surrogate recoveries, retention time windows, detection limits, precision, and accuracy. Poor equipment performance may appear as a failure in these quality control results. The review procedure should therefore require correlation of instrument maintenance and calibration history with quality control trends. When quality control failures occur repeatedly on a specific instrument, the laboratory should determine whether the cause is equipment-related, method-related, reagent-related, sample-related, or due to operator technique.

Trend review is a useful element of equipment oversight. Instead of evaluating each failure as an isolated event, the laboratory reviews data over time. Trends may include gradual loss of detector sensitivity, increasing calibration drift, repeated high blank values, rising vacuum pressure, reduced balance stability, refrigerator temperature fluctuations, or frequent autosampler misalignment. Trend review may be performed monthly, quarterly, or during scheduled management or technical reviews. The results help identify equipment that remains technically functional but is becoming less reliable. Early identification can prevent missed holding times, reanalysis, reporting delays, and increased corrective action workload.

Equipment that fails a calibration, verification, intermediate check, or performance requirement must be managed under a defined nonconforming equipment process. The equipment is labeled, locked out, or otherwise identified to prevent unintended use. The laboratory assesses the significance of the failure and determines whether previously reported data may have been affected. This assessment considers the nature of the failure, the time since the last acceptable check, the types of tests performed, and the degree to which quality control samples demonstrated acceptable performance. If data are potentially affected, the laboratory evaluates whether clients or regulatory agencies must be notified, whether reports must be amended, and whether samples can be reanalyzed.

The out-of-service process should not be limited to major analytical instruments. A malfunctioning refrigerator, an inaccurate thermometer, a damaged pipette, or an unstable incubator can compromise results just as effectively as a detector failure. The equipment review procedure should state that any employee may identify equipment concerns and initiate removal from service. The responsible supervisor or quality representative then evaluates the issue and determines the appropriate disposition. Return to service requires evidence that the problem has been corrected and that the equipment meets acceptance criteria.

Software and computerized systems require review as part of equipment control. Many environmental testing laboratories use chromatography data systems, laboratory information management systems, instrument control software, automated calculation spreadsheets, and electronic temperature monitoring systems. Software review includes confirming version control, user access, audit trail settings where applicable, backup procedures, data integrity controls, and validation of calculations. Changes to software, methods, macros, instrument parameters, or data processing templates should be reviewed before implementation. The review should verify that changes do not compromise data accuracy, traceability, or compliance with method requirements.

Environmental conditions can affect equipment operation and must be considered during review. Instruments may require stable temperature, humidity control, clean power, gas supplies, ventilation, and adequate bench space. For example, high humidity may affect balances and some detectors, while poor air conditioning may affect mass spectrometer vacuum stability or refrigerator performance. Gas purity and pressure can influence chromatography and spectroscopy. Equipment review should include confirmation that supporting utilities remain suitable. When facility changes occur, such as relocation of instruments, renovation, electrical work, or ventilation changes, affected equipment should be reassessed before routine use resumes.

Records are central to the equipment review process. Each equipment file should contain sufficient information to reconstruct the status and history of the item. Records commonly include purchase information, acceptance checks, calibration certificates, maintenance logs, service reports, intermediate check results, repair records, performance qualification data, software changes, nonconformance evaluations, and retirement documentation. Records must be legible, attributable, dated, and protected from loss or unauthorized alteration. Electronic records should be backed up and controlled. The retention period should meet regulatory, accreditation, contractual, and internal requirements.

Responsibility for equipment review is normally distributed among several roles. Analysts perform routine checks, document use, report abnormalities, and complete basic maintenance. Technical supervisors evaluate performance, approve return to service, and assess data impact. Quality personnel review records for compliance with procedures, verify corrective actions, and participate in audits. Laboratory management provides resources for maintenance, calibration, replacement, and training. The procedure should define these responsibilities clearly enough that delays and gaps are minimized. Ambiguity in responsibility often leads to overdue calibrations, incomplete records, or continued use of equipment with unresolved concerns.

Training is necessary to ensure consistent implementation. Personnel must be trained not only in how to operate instruments but also in how to recognize abnormal behavior and document equipment status. Training should address routine startup checks, shutdown procedures, maintenance tasks, calibration requirements, acceptance criteria, use of logbooks or electronic systems, and the process for reporting malfunctions. Competency may be demonstrated through observation, review of records, successful performance of checks, and understanding of relevant procedures. When new equipment is introduced or when procedures change, affected personnel should receive updated training before independent use.

Internal audits provide an independent review of equipment control. During an audit, selected equipment records are examined to confirm that calibration is current, maintenance is documented, intermediate checks are performed, acceptance criteria are applied, and out-of-service events are handled appropriately. Auditors may compare equipment labels with inventory records, examine whether due dates are visible and accurate, and verify that equipment used in recent analyses was acceptable at the time of use. Audit findings are documented and assigned for correction. Repeated findings may indicate that procedures are unclear, resources are inadequate, or staff training is insufficient.

Management review uses equipment information at a broader level. The laboratory may consider the number of equipment failures, overdue calibration events, service costs, downtime, corrective actions, quality control failures associated with equipment, and capital replacement needs. This review supports planning and budgeting. Older instruments may remain acceptable if they are stable, maintainable, and fit for purpose, but review data may show that replacement is more efficient than continued repair. Management review also allows the laboratory to assess whether equipment capacity is sufficient for sample volume, method scope, and required turnaround times.

Equipment review procedures should address leased, rented, borrowed, or client-provided equipment. Such equipment must be controlled before use in the same manner as owned equipment if it can affect test results. The laboratory should obtain calibration and maintenance records or perform its own verification. Responsibilities for repair, calibration, and record retention should be established before the equipment is used. Temporary equipment can introduce risk because staff may be less familiar with operation and limitations. Additional training and verification may be required.

Field equipment presents additional considerations. Environmental testing laboratories may use meters, pumps, flow calibrators, sample collection devices, coolers, and field filtration equipment outside the controlled laboratory environment. These items are exposed to transport, weather, dust, vibration, and handling by multiple users. Review procedures should include pre-use and post-use checks, battery condition, cleanliness, calibration status, decontamination, and storage. Field meters for pH, conductivity, dissolved oxygen, turbidity, or chlorine often require calibration or verification on the day of use. Records must link field measurements to the specific equipment used.

Cleaning and contamination control are part of equipment review. Instruments and devices used in trace-level environmental analysis can be affected by carryover and background contamination. Review procedures should require appropriate cleaning after use, verification of cleanliness when needed, and investigation of contamination trends. For example, persistent blank contamination in volatile organic analysis may indicate issues with autosampler components, gas lines, water supplies, or sample handling equipment. Metals analysis may be affected by digestion vessels, pipette tips, labware, or reagent delivery systems. The review process should ensure that equipment condition is considered during contamination investigations.

Safety considerations are related but distinct from analytical suitability. Equipment may be analytically functional while presenting unacceptable safety risks. Review procedures should require attention to electrical integrity, pressure systems, compressed gases, vacuum systems, moving parts, high temperatures, ultraviolet light, chemical exposure, and exhaust ventilation. Fume hoods and biological safety cabinets, where present, require periodic certification or verification. If a safety issue is identified, the equipment should not be used until the hazard is controlled. Safety-related inspections and corrective actions should be retained with equipment or facility records as appropriate.

Change control is required when equipment is modified, relocated, repurposed, or used for a new method. A change that appears minor can affect performance. Examples include replacing a detector with a different model, changing sample introduction hardware, installing new software, moving a balance to another bench, connecting an instrument to a different gas supply, or using a refrigerator for a different sample type. The laboratory should evaluate the change before routine use and determine what verification is required. Method validation or verification may be needed when equipment changes affect measurement capability.

Supplier and service provider evaluation supports equipment reliability. Laboratories often depend on external providers for calibration, repair, preventive maintenance, gases, standards, and critical replacement parts. The equipment review process should include criteria for approving and monitoring these providers. Evidence may include accreditation, traceability documentation, technical competence, timely service, quality of reports, and responsiveness to problems. If a provider repeatedly supplies incomplete certificates, incorrect parts, or delayed service, the laboratory should consider corrective action or alternative suppliers.

Effective equipment review is a continuous process rather than a single annual activity. It combines daily observations, scheduled checks, formal record review, trend evaluation, corrective action, and management oversight. The level of control should be proportional to the effect that each item can have on test results, sample integrity, safety, and regulatory compliance. A laboratory with well-implemented procedures can demonstrate that its equipment was suitable at the time of analysis and that problems were identified, evaluated, and corrected in a controlled manner.

The procedure should remain practical. Excessive documentation that does not improve control can reduce compliance and obscure important information. Conversely, inadequate documentation leaves the laboratory unable to defend data quality. The most effective approach is a clear system that identifies equipment, defines required checks, assigns responsibility, records decisions, and links equipment status to analytical data. Periodic review of the procedure itself is necessary to incorporate new methods, new instruments, accreditation changes, and lessons learned from failures. Through this structured approach, equipment review contributes directly to the reliability, traceability, and defensibility of environmental testing results.
