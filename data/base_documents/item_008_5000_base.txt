Environmental testing laboratories rely on a broad range of equipment to generate measurements that are used for regulatory compliance, site assessment, remediation decisions, industrial monitoring, product stewardship, and public health evaluation. The reliability of the reported data depends not only on the competence of staff and the suitability of analytical methods, but also on the continuing fitness of instruments, support systems, sampling devices, and measurement standards. Equipment review procedures provide a controlled process for determining whether equipment is appropriate for its intended use, properly installed, correctly calibrated, maintained in a state of control, and documented with sufficient traceability. The review process is therefore a central element of laboratory quality management.

An environmental testing laboratory typically operates instruments for inorganic chemistry, organic chemistry, microbiology, radiochemistry, air analysis, field sampling support, sample preparation, and general laboratory measurement. The equipment inventory may include gas chromatographs, liquid chromatographs, mass spectrometers, inductively coupled plasma instruments, ion chromatographs, spectrophotometers, balances, incubators, ovens, refrigerators, autoclaves, pH meters, conductivity meters, turbidimeters, dissolved oxygen meters, flow meters, pumps, fume hoods, temperature monitoring devices, sample preservation units, automated extractors, centrifuges, and data acquisition systems. Each item has different operating requirements and different consequences if it fails. Review procedures must account for these differences without creating unnecessary administrative complexity.

The purpose of an equipment review is to provide objective assurance that equipment remains suitable for the analytical or support function assigned to it. This includes confirmation that the equipment can meet method-specific performance criteria, that calibration and maintenance are current, that operational checks are completed, that records are complete, and that personnel understand any restrictions on use. The review also evaluates whether the equipment is still compatible with current laboratory methods, accreditation requirements, safety obligations, and data reporting practices. Where deficiencies are found, the review process provides a mechanism for documenting the condition, assessing data impact, assigning corrective actions, and preventing recurrence.

The procedures described in this report are intended to apply across the equipment lifecycle. That lifecycle begins before purchase, when the laboratory evaluates the technical requirements and operational constraints associated with a proposed item. It continues through receipt, installation, qualification, routine use, periodic verification, maintenance, repair, relocation, software updates, method changes, and eventual retirement. Treating equipment review as a lifecycle activity is important because many failures arise from decisions made before an instrument enters routine service. Inadequate evaluation at procurement can lead to equipment that lacks required sensitivity, cannot handle expected sample matrices, requires utilities that the laboratory cannot reliably provide, or is incompatible with laboratory information systems.

Before acquiring equipment, the laboratory should define the intended use in practical terms. This definition should identify the analytical methods to be supported, the target analytes or parameters, the expected concentration ranges, the anticipated sample throughput, the environmental conditions needed for operation, and the applicable accreditation or regulatory requirements. For example, a system purchased for trace organic analysis in drinking water must be evaluated against different criteria from a system intended for high-level screening of contaminated soil extracts. Similarly, a balance used for analytical standards preparation requires a different level of calibration control than a balance used only for approximate reagent weighing.

The equipment review process at the procurement stage should include participation from technical management, quality assurance, information technology personnel where software connectivity is relevant, safety staff where hazards are present, and analysts who understand routine operational conditions. This review should not be limited to vendor claims. The laboratory should consider method detection limits, instrument stability, available service support, compatibility with existing consumables, anticipated maintenance burden, spare parts availability, operator training requirements, data export capability, cybersecurity risks, physical footprint, ventilation demand, waste generation, and total cost of ownership. A documented procurement review provides evidence that the laboratory made a reasoned selection.

Upon receipt, equipment should be inspected to verify that it matches the purchase specification, has not been damaged during shipment, and includes required manuals, software, accessories, certificates, and service documentation. The laboratory should assign a unique equipment identifier before the item is placed into use. The identifier should be durable and should be recorded in the equipment inventory. The inventory record should include the manufacturer, model, serial number, laboratory location, date received, responsible department, intended use, criticality classification, calibration requirements, maintenance requirements, software version if applicable, and current status. The status should clearly indicate whether the equipment is in service, restricted, out of service, under repair, pending qualification, or retired.

Installation review confirms that the equipment has been set up in an environment suitable for its intended function. For analytical instruments, this may include verification of electrical supply, grounding, gas purity, carrier gas pressure, exhaust, cooling water, compressed air, humidity control, bench stability, and adequate space for safe operation and maintenance. For incubators, refrigerators, ovens, and freezers, installation review includes placement away from heat sources, adequate airflow, proper loading capacity, and monitoring device placement. For balances, the review includes vibration control, air movement, leveling, and temperature stability. If installation is performed by a vendor, the laboratory should retain service reports and should review them for completeness rather than treating the vendor record as automatically sufficient.

Qualification of equipment before use is an essential part of the review procedure. The laboratory should establish acceptance criteria before testing begins. Qualification may be divided into installation confirmation, operational verification, and performance verification, although the terminology may vary by organization. The main objective is to demonstrate that the equipment functions as required under actual laboratory conditions. This may include leak checks, temperature mapping, wavelength accuracy checks, mass calibration checks, flow verification, autosampler precision, detector response, signal-to-noise assessment, carryover testing, baseline stability, extraction efficiency, sterilization verification, or comparison with an established instrument. Qualification records should state the tests performed, the acceptance criteria, the results, deviations, corrective actions, and approval for use.

Not all equipment requires the same level of review. A risk-based approach is generally appropriate, provided that the criteria are documented and consistently applied. Critical equipment is equipment whose malfunction could directly affect the validity of test results, sample integrity, regulatory compliance, or safety. Examples include analytical instruments used for final measurements, balances used for standard preparation, temperature-controlled storage used for holding preserved samples, incubation units used for microbiological methods, and autoclaves used for sterilization. Noncritical equipment may include devices used for general support where failure would be readily apparent and would not directly affect reported data. Even noncritical items should be managed, but the frequency and depth of review may be reduced.

Calibration is a primary component of equipment review. The laboratory should identify which items require calibration, the required calibration interval, the reference standards to be used, the acceptance criteria, and the person or organization authorized to perform the calibration. Calibration should be traceable to recognized standards where such traceability is available. For equipment calibrated by external providers, the laboratory should verify that the provider is competent and that certificates contain sufficient information. A calibration certificate should identify the equipment, the calibration date, the method used, the standards used, the results as found and as left where applicable, measurement uncertainty when required, and any limitations on use. The laboratory should review certificates before returning equipment to service.

A common weakness in equipment programs is reliance on a certificate without evaluating whether the results meet laboratory needs. The equipment review procedure should require technical assessment of calibration results. If a thermometer, balance, pipette, or flow device is within the provider’s tolerance but outside the tolerance required by a laboratory method, it may still be unsuitable for the intended use. Conversely, if a device is slightly outside a broad manufacturer specification but still suitable for a limited laboratory application, the laboratory may be able to restrict its use with appropriate documentation. The key requirement is that the decision be documented, technically justified, and communicated to users.

Calibration intervals should be established based on manufacturer recommendations, method requirements, regulatory expectations, historical performance, frequency of use, environmental stress, and the consequences of failure. Intervals should not remain static if evidence indicates a need for change. Equipment that repeatedly drifts near tolerance limits may require shorter intervals, additional intermediate checks, repair, or replacement. Equipment with a long history of stability may be considered for interval extension if allowed by applicable standards and if supported by data. Any change in interval should be approved through the quality system and reflected in the equipment inventory or calibration schedule.

Intermediate checks are used to provide assurance between formal calibrations. These checks are especially important for equipment that is used frequently or is subject to drift. Examples include daily balance checks using working weights, pH meter verification with buffers, temperature checks for refrigerators and incubators, continuing calibration verification for analytical instruments, mass axis checks for mass spectrometers, wavelength checks for spectrophotometers, and flow checks for air sampling equipment. The acceptance criteria for intermediate checks should be defined in advance. Failed checks should trigger immediate evaluation of equipment status and potential impact on data generated since the last acceptable check.

Maintenance review is another major element of equipment control. Preventive maintenance helps reduce unexpected failures, extends equipment life, and supports consistent analytical performance. The maintenance program should identify tasks, frequencies, responsible personnel, required parts, safety precautions, and documentation requirements. Preventive maintenance may include cleaning, lubrication, replacement of seals, liners, septa, pump oil, filters, lamps, columns, tubing, injection ports, detector components, autosampler syringes, vacuum components, and temperature sensors. For refrigerators and freezers, maintenance may include cleaning condenser coils, checking door seals, verifying alarms, and removing ice accumulation. For incubators and ovens, it may include cleaning, airflow checks, and verification of set point stability.

Maintenance records should be sufficiently detailed to support later review. A record that merely states “maintenance performed” is usually inadequate. The record should indicate what was done, when it was done, who performed it, which parts were replaced, whether the equipment was removed from service, whether post-maintenance verification was completed, and whether the equipment was returned to unrestricted use. Vendor maintenance reports should be retained and reviewed for any unresolved observations. If vendor work changes instrument settings, software configuration, firmware, calibration factors, or hardware components that could influence data, the laboratory should perform appropriate verification before using the equipment for reportable results.

Repairs require additional scrutiny because they often occur after a failure or suspected failure. When equipment is found to be malfunctioning, it should be clearly identified as out of service or restricted in use. This can be done through physical labeling, electronic status control, or both. The review procedure should require documentation of the problem, the date observed, the person identifying the problem, the affected equipment, and immediate actions taken to prevent unintended use. If the equipment had been used to generate data before the problem was discovered, a data impact assessment should be initiated.

A data impact assessment should consider the nature of the malfunction, the time period during which the malfunction may have existed, the methods and sample batches affected, quality control results, calibration results, maintenance history, analyst observations, and any external evidence such as client complaints or proficiency testing results. The assessment should be performed by qualified technical personnel and reviewed by quality assurance. Outcomes may include no impact, qualification of results, reanalysis of samples, amendment of reports, client notification, regulatory notification where required, or invalidation of data. The rationale for the decision should be documented. The equipment should not be returned to routine service until the cause has been addressed and acceptable performance has been demonstrated.

Environmental testing laboratories must also review equipment used outside the permanent laboratory facility. Field meters, sampling pumps, flow controllers, temperature probes, data loggers, and portable analyzers may experience more variable conditions than bench instruments. They may be exposed to moisture, dust, temperature extremes, vibration, chemical contamination, and rough handling. Review procedures for field equipment should address pre-deployment checks, calibration or verification before use, transport protection, chain of custody for field records, battery status, decontamination, post-use checks, and documentation of field conditions. When field measurements are used as reportable data, the same principles of traceability and fitness for use apply.

Temperature-controlled equipment deserves specific attention because sample integrity can be affected by storage conditions before analysis. Refrigerators, freezers, incubators, water baths, ovens, and sample transport coolers should be monitored using devices appropriate to the required range and tolerance. The placement of monitoring probes should be justified, and temperature mapping should be considered for units with significant internal variation or critical applications. Alarm systems should be tested periodically, and response procedures should be defined. If a storage unit exceeds its allowable range, the laboratory should evaluate affected samples, standards, reagents, and media. The evaluation should consider duration of the excursion, magnitude of deviation, stability requirements, and method-specific holding conditions.

Balances are also a frequent focus of equipment review because they influence standards, reagents, sample portions, and quality control materials. The review procedure should confirm that balances are installed in suitable locations, calibrated at appropriate intervals, checked before use or daily during use, and maintained in clean condition. Working weights should have appropriate traceability and should be handled to prevent damage or contamination. Acceptance criteria should match the balance application. A balance used for weighing low-mass pesticide standards requires tighter control than one used for weighing bulk drying agents. If a balance fails a check, the laboratory should evaluate weighings performed since the last acceptable verification.

Volumetric equipment, including pipettes, burettes, dispensers, and automated dilutors, should be included in the review program when used for quantitative measurements. Verification may include gravimetric checks, vendor calibration, leak testing, precision assessment, and observation of technique. Adjustable pipettes should be checked at relevant volumes rather than only at nominal maximum capacity. Automated liquid handling systems should be assessed for accuracy, precision, carryover, and correct programming. Where disposable tips, syringes, or tubing affect performance, lot changes or substitutions may require additional verification. Documentation should link the device to the method or preparation activity in which it is used.

Analytical instruments typically require both equipment-level review and method-level quality control. A gas chromatograph-mass spectrometer, for example, may pass a tune check but still fail method calibration criteria. Conversely, method quality control may pass while an underlying maintenance issue is developing. Equipment review should therefore integrate instrument maintenance, calibration records, software configuration, instrument logs, sequence checks, quality control performance, and analyst observations. Routine review of trends can identify deterioration before formal failure occurs. Examples include increasing background contamination, declining sensitivity, unstable internal standard response, increasing pump pressure, retention time drift, vacuum instability, or repeated corrective maintenance.

Computerized systems associated with equipment are part of the equipment review process. Modern environmental laboratories depend on instrument control software, chromatography data systems, mass spectral libraries, laboratory information management systems, electronic notebooks, networked temperature monitoring systems, and automated data transfer applications. Software affects data acquisition, processing, integration, calculation, reporting, audit trails, user access, and record retention. The laboratory should maintain records of software versions, user permissions, configuration settings, calculation templates, backup procedures, and changes. Validation or verification should demonstrate that the software performs intended functions correctly. Changes to software should be controlled, tested, approved, and documented before routine use.

Data integrity considerations should be included in equipment reviews. The laboratory should ensure that electronic records generated by instruments are attributable, legible, contemporaneous, original or true copies, accurate, complete, consistent, enduring, and available. User accounts should be assigned to individuals rather than shared where feasible. Audit trails should be enabled when supported by the system, and review procedures should define when audit trail review is required. Data files should be protected against unauthorized alteration or deletion. Backup and recovery processes should be tested periodically. Equipment that produces only paper records should have procedures to ensure records are retained, linked to the correct samples, and protected from loss or deterioration.

The review of equipment logs is a practical method for detecting recurring problems. Logs may be electronic or paper-based, but they should be accessible to users and reviewers. The log should identify usage, maintenance, malfunctions, adjustments, service visits, performance checks, and restrictions. For complex instruments, sequence logs and run records may supplement the equipment log. Technical supervisors should periodically review logs for completeness and for evidence of unresolved problems. Repeated minor issues, such as intermittent pressure fluctuations or occasional communication failures, should not be ignored simply because they do not immediately invalidate data. Patterns may indicate a developing failure.

Staff qualification is closely related to equipment review. Equipment can be technically suitable but still produce unreliable results if operated by untrained personnel. Procedures should define training requirements for operation, routine maintenance, troubleshooting, calibration checks, and data review. Training should include the specific instrument configuration in use, not only general theory. Competency should be documented through observation, successful performance of practice analyses, review of quality control results, or other appropriate evidence. When equipment is significantly modified, repaired, or replaced, users may require additional training. Access to certain equipment may be restricted to trained personnel, especially where improper operation could damage the equipment or compromise data.

The laboratory should establish periodic comprehensive equipment reviews in addition to routine daily or batch-level checks. The frequency may vary based on criticality, but annual review is common for major equipment. A comprehensive review should evaluate the inventory record, calibration status, maintenance history, repair history, intermediate checks, quality control trends, software status, user access, open deviations, environmental conditions, and continued suitability for current methods. The review should also determine whether the equipment is underused, overloaded, nearing obsolescence, or requiring replacement. The output should be a documented conclusion, including any actions needed.

Quality assurance personnel have an oversight role in equipment review, but responsibility should not rest solely with the quality unit. Technical departments are responsible for operating and maintaining equipment in a controlled manner. Analysts are responsible for completing checks, recording observations, and reporting problems. Supervisors are responsible for ensuring that procedures are followed and that equipment is not used outside approved conditions. Quality assurance is responsible for independent review, system monitoring, audit, and confirmation that corrective actions are effective. Clear assignment of responsibilities reduces the likelihood that equipment issues will be overlooked because each group assumes another group is managing them.

Internal audits should include evaluation of equipment control. Auditors should compare the equipment inventory with equipment physically present in the laboratory, verify that status labels are accurate, review calibration and maintenance records, examine out-of-service controls, check whether users understand restrictions, and trace selected data packages back to the equipment used. Audits should also test the effectiveness of scheduling systems by identifying whether any calibrations or maintenance activities were overdue during the audit period. Findings should be documented, classified according to severity, and addressed through the corrective action system. Audit results should be considered during management review.

External assessments by accreditation bodies, regulatory agencies, and clients often focus on equipment records because they provide objective evidence of laboratory control. Deficiencies may include missing calibration certificates, incomplete maintenance logs, use of equipment past calibration due date, inadequate documentation of repairs, lack of data impact assessment, uncontrolled software changes, and failure to restrict defective equipment. The laboratory should prepare for external assessments by maintaining records in an organized manner and by ensuring that staff can explain how equipment status is determined. Preparation should not consist of retrospective record creation. Records should reflect contemporaneous control.

Corrective action procedures apply when equipment review identifies nonconformities. The corrective action should address both the immediate issue and the underlying cause. For example, if a refrigerator temperature check was missed, the immediate action may be to check the temperature and evaluate stored samples. The root cause analysis may determine that responsibility was unclear during weekend shifts, that the monitoring form was not available, or that the alarm notification list was outdated. Corrective action may include revised schedules, electronic alerts, staff training, or changes in monitoring technology. Effectiveness should be verified after implementation.

Preventive action and continual improvement are also supported by equipment review. Trend analysis can indicate where failures are likely to occur. If several instruments of the same model require repeated pump repairs, the laboratory may revise maintenance intervals, stock critical spare parts, request vendor evaluation, or plan replacement. If multiple analysts make similar documentation errors in equipment logs, the forms may be redesigned. If temperature excursions occur during power interruptions, the laboratory may install backup power or relocate critical storage units. The review process should not be limited to assigning blame after failures. It should provide information for improving system reliability.

Change control is required when equipment changes could affect analytical results or sample integrity. Changes may include installation of new instruments, relocation of existing equipment, replacement of critical components, software upgrades, firmware changes, new columns or detectors, revised integration parameters, modified temperature settings, new gas supplies, or changes to maintenance intervals. The laboratory should assess the potential impact before implementation where possible. Verification testing should be proportionate to the change. Relocating a balance may require leveling and performance checks. Replacing a mass spectrometer detector may require tuning, calibration, and method performance verification. Upgrading chromatography software may require testing of calculations, audit trails, data processing, and report outputs.

Equipment review procedures should address consumables and auxiliary materials when they influence equipment performance. Analytical instruments rely on gases, solvents, reagents, standards, columns, traps, filters, tubing, vials, septa, liners, and syringes. Poor-quality consumables can appear to be equipment failure or can cause actual damage. The laboratory should maintain controls for critical consumables, including receipt inspection, lot verification where required, storage conditions, expiration dates, and compatibility with equipment. When unusual contamination or poor performance occurs, the investigation should consider recent consumable changes. Records should allow the laboratory to identify which lots were in use during affected analyses when such linkage is relevant.

Safety is not separate from equipment review. Malfunctioning or poorly maintained equipment may create risks such as electrical hazards, high-pressure gas leaks, chemical exposure, fire, burns, cuts, vacuum implosion, biological contamination, ultraviolet exposure, or ergonomic injury. Review procedures should confirm that safety features are functional and that equipment is operated within design limits. Fume hoods, biosafety cabinets, gas cabinets, emergency shutoff systems, autoclaves, and pressure vessels may require specialized inspection or certification. Maintenance activities should include lockout or isolation where applicable. Equipment should not be returned to service if safety concerns remain unresolved, even if analytical performance appears acceptable.

Environmental conditions within the laboratory can influence equipment performance. Temperature, humidity, vibration, dust, airflow, electromagnetic interference, and power stability may affect measurements. Equipment review should consider whether the laboratory environment remains suitable. This is particularly important after building modifications, relocation of instruments, changes in heating and ventilation systems, installation of new equipment that generates heat, or recurring power disturbances. Environmental monitoring records can be useful in evaluating unexpected instrument behavior. For example, balance instability may be related to airflow changes, while chromatographic retention shifts may be associated with room temperature variation. Corrective actions may involve facility changes rather than instrument repair.

The equipment inventory should be controlled as a quality record. It should be updated promptly when equipment is added, moved, restricted, repaired, modified, or retired. A laboratory with a large inventory may use a computerized asset management system to schedule calibration and maintenance, store records, generate alerts, and track status. The system itself should be controlled to prevent unauthorized changes and to preserve historical records. If spreadsheets are used, they should be protected against accidental alteration and should be backed up. The inventory should not include only major instruments. Support equipment that can affect results should be included at a level appropriate to its significance.

Status identification is a simple but important control. Users should be able to determine whether equipment is available for use without searching through unrelated records. Status labels may include in-service, calibration due date, out of service, restricted use, calibration not required, or for training only. Electronic systems may supplement labels, but physical labels remain useful in busy laboratories. Labels should be legible, current, and attached in a way that does not interfere with operation or cleaning. When equipment is out of service, the label should identify the restriction clearly. If possible, defective equipment should be physically segregated or disabled to prevent accidental use.

Records generated during equipment review should be retained for a period consistent with regulatory, accreditation, contractual, and internal requirements. Retention periods may need to cover the time during which related analytical data must be defensible. Records should include procurement evaluations where relevant, installation records, qualification records, calibration certificates, maintenance logs, repair records, verification checks, software validation records, change control records, audit findings, corrective actions, and retirement documentation. Records may be paper or electronic, but they should be retrievable, protected, and linked to the equipment identifier. If records are transferred between systems, the laboratory should ensure that context and traceability are preserved.

Review procedures should define how equipment is handled when calibration or maintenance is overdue. Automatic extension of due dates without evaluation is not acceptable. If an interval must be extended, the laboratory should document the reason, assess risk, review recent performance, and approve the extension before continued use. Some equipment may be removed from service until the activity is completed. If equipment was used after the due date, a nonconformance should be documented and data impact assessed. The assessment should consider whether intermediate checks were acceptable and whether the missed activity could reasonably affect reported results. Repeated overdue events indicate a system failure that requires corrective action.

Equipment used for method development, research, or screening should be clearly distinguished from equipment used for accredited or reportable testing. Development equipment may not require the same controls if its output is not used for client reporting, but the distinction must be clear. If data from development equipment are later used to support method validation or client decisions, appropriate review and documentation become necessary. Similarly, instruments used for preliminary screening may influence decisions about dilution, extraction, or selection of analytical method. The laboratory should define when screening equipment requires calibration, verification, and records comparable to routine analytical equipment.

Subcontracted services can affect equipment control. External calibration providers, maintenance vendors, software support providers, and leased equipment suppliers should be evaluated according to the significance of the service. The laboratory should define requirements in purchase orders or service agreements, including documentation expectations, notification of changes, use of traceable standards, confidentiality, and access to records where needed. After service is performed, the laboratory should review the documentation and verify that the equipment is acceptable for use. Vendor statements should not replace laboratory approval. If a vendor identifies a serious fault, the laboratory remains responsible for assessing the impact on its own data.

Leased, borrowed, or demonstration equipment should be subject to review before use for reportable work. The laboratory should obtain sufficient information about the equipment’s history, calibration status, maintenance condition, software configuration, and suitability. Demonstration instruments may be configured to show optimal performance under limited conditions and may not be appropriate for routine samples without additional evaluation. Borrowed field meters or temporary replacement instruments should be labeled, entered into the equipment control system, and verified before use. The temporary nature of the arrangement does not remove the need for traceability.

Decommissioning and retirement are part of equipment review. Equipment may be retired because it is obsolete, unreliable, unsupported by the manufacturer, damaged beyond economical repair, no longer needed, or replaced by improved technology. Before retirement, the laboratory should ensure that data and records stored on the equipment are preserved or transferred appropriately. Computers and data storage devices should be handled according to data security requirements. Hazardous contamination should be evaluated before disposal or transfer. The inventory should be updated to show the retirement date and disposition. Retired equipment should be removed from service areas or clearly marked to prevent unintended use.

Equipment review also supports continuity planning. Environmental laboratories may be required to maintain operations during high sample demand, emergency response events, or public health incidents. Critical equipment failure during such periods can delay decisions. The review process should identify single points of failure, such as one instrument supporting a high-volume regulated method or one freezer storing critical standards. Contingency plans may include backup instruments, service contracts, spare parts, cross-training, mutual aid arrangements with other laboratories, or priority repair agreements. The adequacy of contingency plans should be reviewed periodically, especially when workload or method scope changes.

Measurement uncertainty and method performance are related to equipment condition. Although environmental testing often relies on method-defined quality control rather than explicit uncertainty statements for every result, equipment variability contributes to overall measurement uncertainty. Balances, volumetric devices, temperature systems, detectors, flow controllers, and calibration standards all influence precision and bias. Equipment review records provide evidence for evaluating whether uncertainty estimates remain valid. If equipment performance changes significantly, the laboratory may need to reassess method detection limits, reporting limits, calibration models, precision, bias, or other validation parameters. This is particularly important after major repairs or replacement of critical components.

The review procedure should define acceptance of equipment for specific methods rather than assuming universal suitability. A single instrument may be suitable for one method but not another. For example, a chromatograph may meet performance criteria for high-level petroleum hydrocarbon analysis but lack sensitivity for low-level drinking water contaminants. A furnace may maintain a temperature range adequate for drying glassware but not for a method requiring tightly controlled solids determination. The equipment record should either define approved uses or link to method records that identify acceptable equipment. Restrictions should be visible to users and should be maintained when equipment is moved or repurposed.

Sample preparation equipment requires careful review because preparation errors may not be detected by final instrument checks. Digesters, extraction systems, shakers, mills, homogenizers, filtration assemblies, centrifuges, evaporation systems, and microwave digestion units can affect recovery and representativeness. Review should include operating parameters such as temperature, pressure, time, speed, agitation, solvent volume, and program settings. Automated systems should be checked for correct sequence operation and dispensing accuracy. Equipment used to reduce particle size or homogenize samples should be evaluated for contamination, cleaning effectiveness, and cross-sample carryover. If preparation equipment fails, the affected sample batches may need to be reprocessed if holding times and sample availability allow.

Microbiology equipment review has additional considerations. Incubators, water baths, autoclaves, biological safety cabinets, colony counters, membrane filtration units, media preparation equipment, and refrigerators directly influence microbial recovery and contamination control. Incubation temperatures and times must meet method requirements. Autoclave performance should be verified using appropriate indicators and records of cycle parameters. Sterility checks, media performance checks, and environmental monitoring can provide evidence of equipment control. Because microbial results may be highly sensitive to time and temperature, deviations should be assessed promptly. Equipment cleaning and disinfection procedures should be documented and verified where appropriate.

Air testing and emissions support equipment may include sampling trains, pumps, dry gas meters, critical orifices, mass flow controllers, sorbent tube samplers, canister systems, regulators, and portable analyzers. These devices often require calibration or verification before and after sampling events. Flow accuracy, leak integrity, material compatibility, and cleanliness are important. Canisters and sampling lines may require cleaning certification and vacuum checks. Field conditions should be recorded because temperature, pressure, humidity, and particulate loading can affect performance. Equipment review should ensure that field and laboratory records can be connected, allowing the laboratory to verify that sampling equipment was acceptable for the data reported.

Radiochemistry or low-level trace analysis equipment may require enhanced contamination control. Detectors, counters, shields, clean benches, digestion systems, and sample containers may have background contributions that affect detection capability. Review procedures should include background checks, efficiency calibration, energy calibration, contamination surveys, and control charts where applicable. Equipment used in clean areas should be maintained to prevent introduction of contaminants. Repairs or component changes may alter background or efficiency and therefore require requalification. Documentation should be sufficient to reconstruct the measurement conditions for reported results.

Control charts and trend tools are useful in equipment review. They allow the laboratory to distinguish random variation from systematic change. Control charts may be used for balance checks, temperature records, continuing calibration verification, instrument sensitivity, retention time, blank contamination, detector background, or reference material recovery. The procedure should define warning and action limits, review frequency, and required response. Trend review should not be limited to values outside acceptance limits. A consistent movement toward a limit may warrant investigation before failure occurs. Effective trending requires that data be entered consistently and reviewed by personnel who understand the measurement process.

Management review should include information from equipment reviews. Senior laboratory management is responsible for ensuring that sufficient resources are available for equipment maintenance, calibration, replacement, facility support, and staff training. Metrics may include overdue calibration events, equipment downtime, repeat repairs, audit findings, corrective actions, service costs, capacity constraints, and instrument utilization. Management review should consider whether existing equipment supports the laboratory’s scope of accreditation, client commitments, and strategic plans. Decisions to defer replacement or maintenance should be documented with awareness of risk. Equipment control cannot be sustained if the laboratory lacks resources to address known problems.

The culture of equipment control is also significant. Analysts should feel responsible for reporting abnormal sounds, odors, response patterns, leaks, error messages, and other signs of malfunction. Supervisors should respond to such reports without discouraging communication. A laboratory that treats equipment problems as routine inconveniences may gradually normalize unacceptable conditions. Conversely, a laboratory that requires formal investigation for every minor observation may create excessive burden and discourage reporting. Procedures should support proportionate response. Clear criteria for escalation help staff determine when an observation can be handled through routine maintenance and when it requires nonconformance or corrective action.

The equipment review procedure should be written in a manner that is specific enough to be enforceable but flexible enough to apply to varied equipment. It should define terms, responsibilities, lifecycle stages, inventory requirements, calibration control, maintenance control, verification checks, status identification, software control, record requirements, nonconformance handling, data impact assessment, change control, and retirement. Method-specific procedures may provide additional detail for particular instruments. Forms and electronic systems should support the procedure rather than duplicate or contradict it. Periodic review of the procedure itself is necessary to ensure continued alignment with standards, regulations, and actual laboratory practice.

Implementation should include communication and training. Staff should understand not only what records to complete but why the records are required. Training should use examples relevant to the laboratory, such as a failed balance check, a refrigerator excursion, a mass spectrometer repair, or an overdue pipette calibration. Personnel should know how to determine equipment status, where to find records, how to report problems, and when to stop using equipment. New staff should be trained before independent equipment use. Experienced staff should receive refresher training when procedures change or when audit findings indicate misunderstanding.

The effectiveness of equipment review can be evaluated through several indicators. Equipment should be available when needed, calibration and maintenance should be completed on schedule, records should be complete and retrievable, data impact assessments should be timely and technically justified, repeat failures should decrease or be explained, and audit findings should be addressed. Quality control failures should not repeatedly arise from preventable equipment issues. Client complaints related to equipment should be rare and investigated. Proficiency testing performance may also provide indirect evidence, although satisfactory proficiency results do not by themselves prove that equipment control is adequate.

A well-structured equipment review procedure does not eliminate all failures. Instruments will still malfunction, components will wear, software will require updates, and unexpected conditions will occur. The objective is to detect problems early, prevent use of unsuitable equipment, preserve traceability, and support defensible decisions when deviations occur. In an environmental testing laboratory, where reported results may influence compliance actions, cleanup decisions, permits, and risk evaluations, equipment control is a practical requirement rather than a purely administrative activity.

The overall conclusion is that equipment review should be integrated with the laboratory’s quality system, technical operations, data integrity controls, and management planning. It should begin before purchase and continue until final disposal. It should be based on documented requirements, risk to data quality, objective evidence, and timely review by competent personnel. The procedures should produce records that demonstrate fitness for use and provide a basis for corrective action when needed.
